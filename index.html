Beechui (Katy) Koo

University of Chicago. MS in Applied Data Science.

I am a Master’s student in Applied Data Science at the University of Chicago, with a strong background in machine learning and biology. Currently, I work as a Research Associate in the Department of Radiation and Cellular Oncology at the University of Chicago Medicine, advised by Dr. James Sohn, and as a Data Scientist/AI Engineer at Oncosoft Inc.

Since my undergraduate years, I have been engaged in research at Boston Children’s Hospital and UChicago Medicine, contributing to projects spanning the quantitative analysis of metal artifact reduction in radiation oncology and the investigation of stress granules in immunological contexts.

Alongside my research, I bring several years of academic and professional experience as an AI engineer and software engineer in biotech companies, working on projects involving machine learning models with single-cell flow cytometry data, large language models (LLMs), and multimodal AI systems.

selected publications

  1. RPC
    Quantitative Comparison of CT Metal Artifact Reduction (MAR) Algorithms in Radiation Oncology
    Beechui Koo, Hyunuk Jung, Mitchell Polizzi, and 3 more authors
    Radiation Physics and Chemistry, 2025
    Minor Revision Submitted
    Abs Bib

    Metal artifacts in computed tomography (CT) can severely degrade image quality and compromise dose calculation accuracy in radiation oncology. This study presents a quantitative comparison of four commercial metal artifact reduction (MAR) algorithms—SEMAR (Canon), Smart-MAR (GE), O-MAR (Philips), and iMAR (Siemens)—using a standardized phantom-based evaluation. A custom resin phantom containing interchangeable inserts (solid water, aluminum, titanium, and stainless steel) was scanned on each system, and proprietary MAR algorithms were applied. Quantitative analysis included (1) circular profile assessment of mean CT number (CTN) deviations, (2) geometric accuracy evaluation of metallic inserts, and (3) volumetric artifact quantification via a composite M Error Index integrating artifact extent and intensity. All MAR methods showed reduced |ΔCTN| at mid-to-far distances from metal interfaces, but notable residual deviations near metal boundaries. SEMAR and iMAR demonstrated the most consistent geometric accuracy (<5%) and lowest M Error values (0.00–0.83), while O-MAR exhibited higher deviations (1.15–2.46). Smart-MAR showed the largest CTN errors for aluminum and stainless steel. Overall, this study establishes a quantitative benchmarking framework for cross-vendor MAR performance, offering objective metrics to inform system selection and improve confidence in CT-based treatment planning for patients with metal implants.

  2. Mol Cell
    Stress granules are shock absorbers that prevent excessive innate immune responses to dsRNA
    Max Paget, Cristhian Cadena, Sadeem Ahmad, and 8 more authors
    Molecular Cell, 2023
    Abs DOI Bib

    Proper defense against microbial infection depends on the controlled activation of the immune system. This is particularly important for the RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs), which recognize viral dsRNA and initiate antiviral innate immune responses with the potential of triggering systemic inflammation and immunopathology. Here, we show that stress granules (SGs), molecular condensates that form in response to various stresses including viral dsRNA, play key roles in the controlled activation of RLR signaling. Without the SG nucleators G3BP1/2 and UBAP2L, dsRNA triggers excessive inflammation and immune-mediated apoptosis. In addition to exogenous dsRNA, host-derived dsRNA generated in response to ADAR1 deficiency is also controlled by SG biology. Intriguingly, SGs can function beyond immune control by suppressing viral replication independently of the RLR pathway. These observations thus highlight the multi-functional nature of SGs as cellular “shock absorbers” that converge on protecting cell homeostasis by dampening both toxic immune response and viral replication.
